Feeding
Twice a day — breakfast (~7 AM) and dinner (~5 PM).
Each meal is 2 cups of raw meat. Sometimes add a little fish oil, and sometimes 2 slices of boiled sweet potato on top of the raw meat.
Defrosting: when the current meat container looks like it's down to about the last meal, take the next container out of the freezer and put it in the sink overnight to defrost. Once defrosted, keep it refrigerated.
Running low? Harmony Farms. Buy only beef and pork — never poultry (poultry gives him diarrhea).

Getting to know Nuvi
A friendly, high-energy young Lab (about 2 years old). Loves people most of all, and gets on well with other dogs.
🗣️
Commands he knows
"Bed" = go into his crate. He also knows sit, paw, heel, off, and leave — and his release word is "go."
🛋️
House manners
Keep food off counters and low tables — he'll help himself. He's allowed on the large chairs and sofas in the living room, and on the dining chairs when they're pulled away from the table.
⏳
Being left alone
He can be home alone from about 9 AM to 2 PM. Whenever nobody is home, latch the back door so he can't get outside. Never leave him outside unattended (coyotes).
👀
What gets him excited
Delivery people (excited in a happy way), and animal scents — bear, coyote, bobcat. Keep a firm hold on the leash when he catches a scent.
🦴
Toys — please read
Loves his ball, the thrower, and a game of tug. He's not protective of food or toys. But do not give him any new toys — he tends to rip things apart and swallow them. He's already had three surgeries to remove objects from his gut (his intestine is now about a foot shorter). No soft toys, no small toys, ever.
🚪
Doors & gates
He doesn't bolt much, but he can sneak out — so always check gates are latched and doors are shut behind you.

He seems sick or hurt
Vomiting, ongoing diarrhea, limping, very low energy, or a swollen/hard belly → call the day vet (CCPH); after hours go to VCA Animal Specialty Group (24h). For mild diarrhea, see the feeding note (half portion + pumpkin, or rice & boiled chicken).
☠️
He ate something he shouldn't
Chocolate, grapes/raisins, xylitol, rat bait, a mushroom, or trash → call ASPCA Poison Control (888) 426-4435 and the vet. This is exactly why he wears the muzzle on trails.
🐍
Snake bite
Rattlesnakes live in these foothills. If bitten (yelp, sudden swelling, puncture marks) → straight to VCA Animal Specialty Group, call ahead. He's vaccinated against rattlesnake bites, but he still needs monitoring and a call to the vet.
🏃
He got out / ran off
Call his name calmly — don't chase, he may think it's a game. Check the gates. Call Jan/Vilay right away. He's microchipped (956000017638680) and tagged; contact Pasadena Humane.
🥵
Too hot
Heavy panting, drooling, wobbliness → shade, water, cool (not ice-cold) wet towels, call the vet. Prevent it: this is why early walks are better — walk early morning or late afternoon, and if it's really hot just shorten the walk (totally fine). Test the pavement with your hand and always bring water.
🔥
Wildfire / evacuation
The foothills carry fire risk. If told to evacuate: leash + muzzle Nuvi, grab his food & meds, and go. [ADD: go-bag location & where to meet]. Call Jan/Vilay. Watch local alerts (e.g. the Watch Duty app).
🌎
Earthquake
Drop, cover, hold on. Afterward keep Nuvi leashed (he may be spooked) and check for hazards / a gas smell. The gas shutoff is seismic-automatic. The water shutoff to the house is behind the house, next to the little fence (photo below).
🔌
Power out
The breaker panel is next to Vilay's office — the large sliding wall has to be moved out to reach it. Ask Greg (neighbor) for help. If it's not quickly solved, call Jan/Vilay.

Wildlife — important
Coyotes roam at dawn & dusk — keep Nuvi close and never leave him in the yard alone. Rattlesnakes are on the trails spring–fall (keep him leashed/muzzled, don't let him nose into brush). Bears and the occasional mountain lion pass through — keep trash lids shut. Dry grass hides foxtails; check his paws & ears after hikes.

Heat
Summer afternoons get hot. Walk morning or evening, bring water, and avoid hot asphalt — if it's too hot for your hand, it's too hot for his paws.
